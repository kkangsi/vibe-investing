\begin{abstract}
\noindent This paper redefines the price discrepancy between the Korean exchange Bithumb and the global
exchange Binance using a new measure of the kimchi premium expressed in BTC units, denoted
$\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t)$, accompanied by calibrated simulation evidence on daily series over 2020--2025.
The conventional KRW-denominated premium $\pi^{\text{KRW}}_X(t)$ confounds many forces---exchange rate
dynamics, capital controls, stablecoin premia, and remittance delays---making it difficult to identify
token-specific asymmetric demand shocks. As a remedy, this paper proposes
\[
   \pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t)=\frac{P_X^B/P_{\mathrm{BTC}}^B}{P_X^N/P_{\mathrm{BTC}}^N}-1,
\]
and proves (Proposition 1, FX-noise cancellation) that this measure is invariant to the exchange rate
and to stablecoin-level fluctuations by numerator--denominator cancellation. The empirical objects of
study are BNB (the gas token of BNB Chain, listed on Bithumb on 2021-05-27), Venus (XVS, a BSC DeFi
governance token), and ETH as an ERC-20 baseline. A normalised variance ratio
$\rho_X=\operatorname{Var}[\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X]/\operatorname{Var}[\Delta\log P_X]$
controls for own-price volatility, OLS regressions are reported with Newey--West HAC standard errors,
and asymmetric volatility is estimated with an EGARCH(1,1) model using Student-$t$ innovations.
Simulation findings suggest that (i) the satoshi premium displays roughly half the standard deviation
of the KRW premium, evidencing FX-noise removal; (ii) after normalisation, both BNB ($\rho=1.13$) and
XVS ($\rho=0.82$) exceed the ETH baseline ($\rho=0.42$), tentatively rejecting BSC arbitrage superiority;
(iii) HAC adjustment causes the Korean regulatory dummy to lose significance while the global-shock
dummy remains highly significant ($p<0.001$); (iv) the EGARCH leverage parameter is
$\hat\gamma=-0.073$ ($p<0.05$), supporting the asymmetric volatility hypothesis H2.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} kimchi premium, BTC-num\'eraire, BNB Chain, capital controls,
triangular arbitrage, limits to arbitrage, EGARCH, Newey--West HAC, Bithumb, Binance \\
\textbf{JEL classification:} G14, G15, F31, G18

\section{Introduction}

The Korean cryptocurrency market is partially segmented from global markets owing to a multilayered
regulatory environment that includes the Foreign Exchange Transactions Act, the Specified Financial
Information Act, the FATF Travel Rule, the real-name verified deposit account regime, and a de facto
ban on direct trading by foreign nationals (Makarov \& Schoar, 2020; Choi et al., 2022; Hwang et al.,
2024). The result of this market segmentation is a persistent price divergence between Korean and
non-Korean exchanges---the so-called \emph{kimchi premium}---which reached approximately 50\% during
the 2017--2018 Bitcoin rally (Makarov \& Schoar, 2020; Eom, 2021).

Most prior research measures the kimchi premium in Korean won (KRW), i.e., by converting the Bithumb
(or Upbit) won price of a token $X$ into US dollars (or USDT) at the prevailing spot exchange rate and
comparing it with the foreign-venue dollar price. This conventional measure, however, embeds within
the same scalar quantity several confounding forces: intraday volatility of the KRW/USD exchange rate,
the USDT premium on Bithumb relative to its foreign parity, banking-side remittance delays, and a
capital-control premium. As a consequence it becomes difficult to disentangle ``Korean retail demand
is asymmetrically intense for token $X$'' from ``Korean retail demand for crypto is broadly elevated''.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it formalises a \emph{BTC-num\'eraire premium}
$\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t)$ that uses BTC as an exchange-internal num\'eraire, and proves
(Proposition 1, Section 3) that this measure cancels exchange-rate and capital-control noise by
numerator--denominator cancellation. Second, it formalises the triangular arbitrage structure of
BNB Chain tokens and introduces a \emph{normalised variance ratio} $\rho_X$ to construct a robust
test of the BSC inefficiency hypothesis $\mathrm{H_{BSC}}$ that controls for own-volatility
differences across tokens. Third, it conducts the simulation analysis with Newey--West HAC standard
errors and an EGARCH(1,1) volatility model in order to control for serial correlation and asymmetric
heteroskedasticity.

The empirical objects of study are three tokens simultaneously listed on Bithumb and Binance:
(i) \textbf{BNB}, the gas token of BNB Chain and a top-five-cap asset, listed on Bithumb on May 27,
2021; (ii) \textbf{Venus (XVS)}, a mid-cap BSC DeFi governance token; and (iii) \textbf{ETH}, the
ERC-20 baseline.

\paragraph{Identification strategy: comparison across non-Korean exchanges.}
Is the kimchi premium driven by ``Korean retail irrationality'' or by ``capital-control-induced
arbitrage frictions''? This paper proposes a new identification strategy: \emph{directly compare the
BTC-num\'eraire premium variability across non-Korean exchanges with that of Bithumb-vs-non-Korean
pairs}. Simulation evidence in Section 6.4 shows that the satoshi premium standard deviation among
Binance, OKX, and Bybit is 0.23--0.34\% for ETH (near zero in practice), whereas Bithumb-vs-non-Korean
pairs reach 0.85--0.90\%, i.e., \emph{3 to 4 times larger}, and approximately 7 times larger when
measured in BNB. Among non-Korean exchanges, arbitrageurs face the same risks but encounter no
systemic delay barrier to moving crypto assets; in Korea, the Foreign Exchange Act, Travel Rule, and
KYC asymmetry actively block the arbitrage channel. Therefore the kimchi premium is plausibly
\emph{the price of a regulatory friction}, not a market inefficiency awaiting liquidity.

\paragraph{Quantitative evidence on capital outflow.}
According to data released by the Korea Customs Service (via the office of NA Member Choi Eun-seok)
on October 21, 2025, detected illegal foreign exchange transactions mediated by crypto assets reach
a 2018--2025 cumulative total of approximately 14.2 trillion KRW, including 1{,}157.9 billion KRW
in 2024 and 881.3 billion KRW in January--August 2025 (Section 6.5, Figure~\ref{fig:policy_cost},
Table~\ref{tab:policy_cost}). These are \emph{detected} amounts only and thus a strict lower bound.
So long as the kimchi premium persists, the Korean market provides a structural impetus for
capital outflow, the burden of which is borne by the Korean economy.

\section{Literature Review}

\paragraph{General theory of arbitrage limits.}
Gromb \& Vayanos (2010) organise the general mechanism by which capital-constrained, risk-averse
arbitrageurs fail to immediately eliminate riskless arbitrage opportunities. The Korean kimchi
premium is a prototypical instance of the ``limits to arbitrage'' framework, in which capital
controls and the KYC regime form a structural friction that blocks arbitrageur entry at the source.

\paragraph{Mechanisms of the kimchi premium.}
Makarov \& Schoar (2020) document persistent Bitcoin price divergences across the US, Korea, Japan,
and Europe and attribute them to arbitrage limits induced by capital controls and regulatory
differences. The Korea--US ratio exceeded 15\% on average during December 2017--February 2018 and
reached 40\% on some days. Eom (2021) estimates by GMM that volume and price instability explain
much of the kimchi premium, and Choi et al.\ (2022) argue that capital controls slow the
premium's mean reversion. More recently, Ahn \& Kim (2023) use Bithumb microdata to identify the
investor types driving the premium, finding individual retail to be the principal source of variation.

\paragraph{Arbitrage frictions, external spillovers, regulatory shocks.}
Borri \& Shakhnov (2020) show that Chinese crypto regulation spills over to the Korean market as a
``China shock''. Kim et al.\ (2022) use Bank of Korea administrative data to demonstrate that
remittances to China increase when the kimchi premium widens, suggesting that the premium also
functioned as a channel for repatriating Chinese miners' funds. Hwang et al.\ (2024) document that
the Travel Rule, implemented in March 2022, further constrained inter-exchange arbitrage and
extended the kimchi premium's half-life; this finding is closely related to hypothesis H4 below
(asymmetric response to regulatory shocks).

\paragraph{Pricing factors and microstructure of crypto markets.}
Liu, Tsyvinski \& Wu (2022, \emph{Journal of Finance}) identify common risk factors (market beta,
size, momentum) in the crypto cross-section, allowing separation of idiosyncratic alpha from
systematic risk; the normalised variance ratio $\rho_X$ in this paper is in the same spirit, deflating
premium variance by own-price variance to remove a token-level scale effect. Pieters \& Vivanco
(2017) attribute inter-exchange BTC price differences to liquidity-structural causes rather than
information asymmetry, and Capponi \& Jia (2021) model price discovery between DEX and CEX and the
arbitrage incentives of liquidity providers; their framework provides direct theoretical grounding
for the triangular arbitrage structure in Section 3.3.

\paragraph{Altcoins and exchange-level segmentation.}
Tiger Research (2024) quantify the Korean alt-coin bias, reporting a 1{,}255\% premium on the
AVAIL listing event of July 2024. Kim (2024) defines a ``Korean venue share'' indicator and analyses
its effect on alt-coin returns under a reinforcement-learning framework.

\paragraph{Positioning of this paper.}
This paper recasts the cross-country arbitrage frame of Makarov \& Schoar (2020) as an
\emph{intra-country, inter-token} problem and extends the CEX--DEX price-discovery model of
Capponi \& Jia (2021) to a Bithumb--Binance--PancakeSwap triangle. To the best of our knowledge,
this is the first paper to use BTC explicitly as a num\'eraire for defining a token-level kimchi
premium.

\section{Theoretical Framework}

\subsection{Notation and four measures of the premium}
For token $X$ at time $t$, let
$P_X^{B,\text{KRW}}(t)$ denote the Bithumb KRW price of $X$,
$P_X^{N,\text{USDT}}(t)$ the Binance USDT price,
$E_t$ the KRW/USD reference rate,
$P_{\text{USDT}}^{B,\text{KRW}}(t)$ the Bithumb USDT/KRW pair,
and $P_{X/\text{BNB}}^{\text{DEX}}(t)$ the PancakeSwap $X$/BNB pool price. We define four measures.

\paragraph{(M1) FX-denominated premium.}
\begin{equation}
\pi^{\text{KRW}}_X(t) = \frac{P_X^{B,\text{KRW}}(t)}{P_X^{N,\text{USDT}}(t)\cdot E_t}-1
\label{eq:pi_krw}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(M2) USDT-denominated premium.}
\begin{equation}
\pi^{\text{USDT}}_X(t) = \frac{P_X^{B,\text{KRW}}(t)}{P_X^{N,\text{USDT}}(t)\cdot P_{\text{USDT}}^{B,\text{KRW}}(t)}-1
\label{eq:pi_usdt}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(M3) BTC-num\'eraire premium --- the paper's central measure.}
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t) = \frac{P_X^{B,\text{KRW}}(t)/P_{\text{BTC}}^{B,\text{KRW}}(t)}
                                {P_X^{N,\text{USDT}}(t)/P_{\text{BTC}}^{N,\text{USDT}}(t)} - 1
\;}
\label{eq:pi_btc}
\end{equation}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Proposition 1 (FX-noise cancellation).}
When both Bithumb prices are quoted in the same currency (KRW) and both Binance prices in the same
currency (USDT), $\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t)$ is invariant to $E_t$, to the USDT/KRW premium
$P_{\text{USDT}}^{B,\text{KRW}}(t)$, and to stablecoin-level fluctuations.
\end{quote}

\noindent\textbf{Proof.} The numerator ratio cancels the KRW unit and the denominator ratio cancels
the USDT unit, so $\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X$ contains no currency conversion factor.\hfill$\Box$

\paragraph{(M4) ETH-baseline alpha.}
\begin{equation}
\alpha_X^{\text{ETH}}(t) = \pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t) - \pi^{\text{BTC}}_{\text{ETH}}(t)
\label{eq:alpha}
\end{equation}

BSC DeFi token (e.g., XVS).

\paragraph{Formalising hypothesis $\mathrm{H_{BSC}}$.}
The naive intuition is: ``Because PancakeSwap adds an arbitrage channel, BSC tokens should exhibit
\emph{lower} satoshi-premium variance than ERC-20 tokens.'' A simple variance comparison, however,
confounds token-level own volatility, since BSC DeFi tokens are typically more volatile than ETH.
This paper introduces the \emph{normalised variance ratio}
\begin{equation}
\rho_X \;\equiv\; \frac{\operatorname{Var}[\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X]}{\operatorname{Var}[\Delta\log P_X]},
\label{eq:rho}
\end{equation}
a unit-free indicator of ``how much the satoshi premium moves per unit of own-price movement''. The
hypothesis is restated as
\begin{equation}
\rho_X^{\text{BSC}} < \rho_X^{\text{ERC20}}.
\label{eq:hbsc}
\end{equation}

\section{Testable Hypotheses}

\begin{description}
\item[H1 (Satoshi anchoring).] $\mathbb{E}[\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t)] \approx 0$. A significant non-zero
mean would imply the absence of an X/BTC arbitrage channel inside Bithumb (i.e., inactive BTC pairs).
\item[H2 (Asymmetric volatility).] An EGARCH(1,1) fit to $\Delta\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X$ exhibits a
leverage term $\gamma<0$ at $p<0.05$.
\item[H3 = $\mathrm{H_{BSC}}$ (Normalised BSC superiority).] $\rho_X^{\text{BSC}}<\rho_X^{\text{ERC20}}$.
\item[H4 (Asymmetric regulatory shock).] Korean regulatory shocks push $\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X$ negative;
global shocks move it transiently toward zero or weakly negative.
\item[H5 (Time-stable capital-control premium).]
$\mathbb{E}[\pi^{\text{KRW}}_X(t)]-\mathbb{E}[\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t)]\approx\lambda$, relatively
stable across regimes.
\end{description}

\section{Data and Methodology}

\paragraph{Sample and data nature.}
The analysis uses a synthetic daily series of $T=2{,}192$ observations covering 2020-01-01 to 2025-12-31.
The series is calibrated to stylized facts reported in Makarov \& Schoar (2020), Eom (2021),
Choi et al.\ (2022), and Tiger Research (2024)---in particular the spring 2021 peak, the LUNA and FTX
shocks of 2022, the July 2024 implementation of the Virtual Asset User Protection Act (VAUPA), and
the December 3, 2024 martial-law incident. The quantitative results below are \emph{simulation
evidence}; precise parameter values must be re-estimated on real exchange data.

\paragraph{Calibration mechanism.}
The synthetic series is constructed by the following stochastic procedure (full specification in
Appendix~A, Table~\ref{tab:cal_spec}; moment-matching in Table~\ref{tab:moment_match}).
The KRW/USD rate is generated as a sinusoidal trend plus two Gaussian shock kernels (a 2022-Q4
strong-dollar episode and the December 2024 martial-law episode) plus IID $N(0,4)$ noise.
The log BTC/USDT price is built by linear interpolation across 14 historical anchors plus a cumulative
Gaussian random walk with $\sigma=0.018$; ETH, BNB, and XVS use the same anchor-interpolation scheme
with token-specific volatilities. The KRW premium $\pi^{\text{KRW}}_X(t)$ is the sum of a baseline
drift, eight Gaussian event bumps of the form $h\cdot\exp(-((t-t_0)/\sigma_d)^2)$ (e.g., the April 2021
peak, the April 22, 2021 FSC statement, the November 12, 2022 FTX shock, the March 16, 2024 BTC ATH,
the July 19, 2024 VAUPA step, and the December 3, 2024 martial-law shock at $h=-32\%$), and IID
$N(0,0.006)$ noise. The random seed (\texttt{20260513}) is fixed for full reproducibility.

\paragraph{Target tokens.}
\textbf{BNB} (BNB Chain gas, listed on Bithumb on 2021-05-27, top-five-cap), \textbf{Venus (XVS)}
(BSC DeFi governance), and \textbf{ETH} (ERC-20 baseline).

\paragraph{Empirical model.}
The principal regression equation is
\begin{multline}
\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t) = \beta_0 + \beta_1\pi^{\text{BTC}}_{\text{ETH}}(t) + \beta_2\text{RV}_{\text{BTC}}(t)
                       + \beta_3\log\!\tfrac{V_X^B(t)}{V_X^N(t)} \\
                       + \beta_4\Delta\log P_{\text{BTC}}(t)
                       + \beta_5 D^{\text{KR}}_t + \beta_6 D^{\text{GL}}_t + u_t.
\label{eq:reg}
\end{multline}
To control for serial correlation and heteroskedasticity standard in financial time series, we use
\textbf{Newey--West HAC} standard errors with a Bartlett-kernel truncation
$L=\lfloor 4(N/100)^{2/9}\rfloor$ (Newey \& West, 1987).

To test the asymmetric volatility hypothesis H2, we fit an EGARCH(1,1) to $\Delta\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X$.
The dependent variable is expressed in \emph{percent} ($\times 100$) and winsorised at $\pm 20\%$ to
limit the influence of tail observations before estimation (a consequence discussed in
\S 8 Limitation 3):
\begin{equation}
\log\sigma_t^2 \;=\; \omega + \alpha\!\left(|z_{t-1}|-\sqrt{2/\pi}\right) + \gamma z_{t-1} + \beta\log\sigma_{t-1}^2,
\label{eq:egarch}
\end{equation}
where $z_t=e_t/\sigma_t$ and $e_t=\Delta\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t)-\mu$. We assume Student-$t$ innovations
of degrees-of-freedom $\nu$ and estimate parameters by quasi-MLE. A statistically negative leverage
term $\gamma$ indicates that negative shocks raise conditional volatility more than positive shocks
of equal size, supporting H2.

\paragraph{Key finding --- HAC adjustment changes the conclusion.}
The ETH satoshi-premium coefficient $\beta_1=1.40$ is highly significant under both standard errors
($t_{\text{OLS}}=25.0$, $t_{\text{HAC}}=12.5$), implying that XVS tracks ETH approximately one-for-one
in the satoshi metric. The Korean regulatory dummy $\beta_5$, however, has $t=3.1$ under OLS
($p<0.01$) but $t=1.12$ under HAC---\emph{losing significance}. Korean regulatory events trigger
autocorrelated dynamics over several days, so the OLS independence assumption understates the
standard error. By contrast, the global-shock dummy $\beta_6=-0.019$ remains highly significant
under HAC ($t=-3.81$, $p<0.001$), suggesting that global shocks act on the premium more immediately
with weaker serial correlation. $R^2=0.27$.

\subsection{Cross-exchange comparison --- regulatory cost or market inefficiency?}
\label{sec:cross}

This subsection brings simulation evidence to bear on the paper's identification strategy. The
hypothesis is simple: \emph{directly compare satoshi-premium variability across non-Korean exchanges
with that of Bithumb-vs-non-Korean pairs}. If the kimchi premium were a ``retail mania''
phenomenon, that mania should affect all exchanges. If it is a \emph{Korea-specific capital-control
cost}, only Bithumb-involved pairs should display large variability.

\noindent\textbf{Reading caveat.} The quantitative results below are based on synthetic data. The
OKX and Bybit series are modelled as ``microstructure noise without regulatory friction'' only (see
the caveat in Appendix~A, Table~\ref{tab:cal_spec}). The small offshore-only premium is therefore
\emph{partly} a function of the modelling assumption. Whether OKX and Bybit display exactly
0.2--0.3\% standard deviations in practice cannot be asserted; the results should be read as a
\emph{simulation demonstration} of how the identification hypothesis manifests under a given
calibration. Precise empirical verification is left to follow-up research (Agenda 7, \S 8).

Table~\ref{tab:cross_exchange} and Figure~\ref{fig:cross_exchange} apply the same measure

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Offshore-only satoshi premia are effectively zero.} For ETH: Binance--OKX 0.23\%,
Binance--Bybit 0.25\%, OKX--Bybit 0.34\%. For BNB: 0.22--0.32\%, with $[1\%, 99\%]$ ranges within
$\pm 0.8\%$.
\item \textbf{Bithumb-involved satoshi premia are an order of magnitude larger.} For ETH all three
Bithumb pairs have standard deviations of 0.85--0.90\% (3--4$\times$); for BNB, 1.59--1.63\%
($\approx 7\times$).
\item \textbf{The result is robust to which non-Korean exchange is the counterparty.} The premium
asymmetry is concentrated on the Bithumb side---i.e., in Korea-specific friction.
\end{itemize}

Among non-Korean exchanges, arbitrageurs can move crypto assets without systemic delay, and any
transient price gap is closed within minutes (consistent with Makarov \& Schoar, 2020, Table 3). In
contrast, Korean exchanges are subject to the Foreign Exchange Act, Travel Rule, real-name-verified
deposit accounts, and the foreigner-trading ban, all of which block the arbitrage channel and allow
gaps to persist for days or weeks. The kimchi premium thus appears to be the price of regulatory
segmentation rather than residual market inefficiency.

\subsection{Quantitative evidence on capital outflow --- the policy cost of the premium}
\label{sec:policy}

If the kimchi premium is indeed a regulatory price asymmetry, the asymmetry becomes an
\emph{arbitrage opportunity} for someone, and the associated funds flows must by-pass Korea's
foreign-exchange regime---i.e., they take the form of \emph{illegal foreign exchange transactions}.
This paper documents a structural association between the kimchi premium and detected illegal
forex activity but \emph{does not assert} a clean causal interpretation: crypto-mediated illegal
forex includes property flight, simple remittance-rule avoidance, and money laundering of various
provenance. Premium-driven arbitrage is plausibly \emph{one of the principal} structural drivers
of these channels. The detection statistic should therefore be read as an \emph{upper-bound
indicator} of the capital-outflow behaviour that would have been absent if the premium itself were
absent.

kimchi-premium arbitrage---the figures are best read alongside Section~\ref{sec:cross} as an
\emph{indirect indicator} of the economic burden the premium imposes on the Korean economy.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{The satoshi premium as a measurement tool.}
The most robust finding of this paper is that the BTC-num\'eraire measure has systematically
better statistical properties than the conventional KRW measure. During Korea-specific
fiscal/political shocks like the December 2024 martial-law event, the KRW premium diverges
explosively whereas the satoshi premium remains relatively stable; conversely, the satoshi premium
responds to global shocks such as LUNA (2022.5) and FTX (2022.11). Monitoring \emph{both} measures
allows one to decompose domestic and global shocks.

\paragraph{Microstructure interpretation of $\rho_{\text{BNB}}>\rho_{\text{XVS}}$.}
The observation that BNB ($\rho=1.13$) exceeds XVS ($\rho=0.82$) is not immediately explained by
a DEX-access-barrier hypothesis alone. BNB is a top-five-cap asset with deep liquidity on both
Bithumb and Binance; intuitively, its premium should mean-revert faster and yield a smaller $\rho$.
We offer two complementary hypotheses. (i) \emph{Basis-trading-induced perturbation.} BNB is the
spot leg of Binance perpetuals, so cash-and-carry trading perturbs the Bithumb BNB/KRW price by
small amounts and inflates the numerator (premium variance), in the spirit of the LP-arbitrage
mechanism of Capponi \& Jia (2021). (ii) \emph{Microstructure noise amplification.} BNB's own
price volatility is slightly lower than ETH's, making the normalised numerator more responsive to
small price gaps. The comparison of $\rho_X$ across high- and low-volatility tokens can therefore
generate an asymmetric interpretation; this is the reason we recommend, as follow-up research, a
\emph{duration analysis} of no-arbitrage-band exits (\S 8 Agenda 6). The decisive conclusion is
reserved.

\paragraph{Implications of the HAC adjustment.}
The loss of significance for the Korean regulatory dummy under HAC carries an important
academic and policy lesson: Korean regulatory events generate residual effects on the premium for
days to weeks (the autocorrelation pattern), so simple OLS-based event studies can overstate
statistical significance.

\paragraph{Exchange-listing advisory.}
The fair-premium formula \eqref{eq:fair_premium} can be applied through the following simplified
empirical specification:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\pi}_X^* \approx 1.8\% + 0.6\sigma_X^{\text{wk}} + 1.2\cdot\mathbb{1}_{\text{retail-heavy}} + 0.4\log(V_N/V_B).
\end{equation}
Under normal market conditions, the fair premium for a typical BSC major is approximately
4--6\%, rising to 8--12\% during alt seasons; clearly exceeding this range is quantitative evidence
of inefficiency. We re-emphasise that all parameter values are simulation-based estimates.

\paragraph{Policy implications --- autonomous competition and foreign-access liberalisation.}
\emph{Under the assumption that the present simulation reflects a first-order approximation of the
real market structure}, Sections~\ref{sec:cross} and \ref{sec:policy} together suggest the following
direction of policy.

First, the KYC/AML/CFT framework should be \emph{maintained at its current intensity or
strengthened}. The risk that crypto assets are abused as channels for money laundering or terrorism
financing must be controlled independently of the Travel Rule, in accordance with the global
standard (FATF Recommendation 16).

Second, restrictions on foreign access to Korean exchanges merit \emph{gradual liberalisation}.
The present ban appears to be a key mechanism that blocks arbitrageur entry and sustains the kimchi
premium, with the consequences that (i) Korean retail bears welfare losses through inefficient
prices, (ii) arbitrage-seeking funds exit through misinvoiced remittance and \emph{hwan-chigi}
channels, and (iii) the indirect economic-cost indicator---detected illegal forex over nine
years---reaches approximately 14.2 trillion KRW.

Third, the construction of a KRW-stablecoin infrastructure appears to be a natural complement:
if the Travel Rule blocks the \emph{time} dimension of arbitrage, a KRW stablecoin provides the
\emph{route} dimension within the same KYC standard, opening a legitimate arbitrage channel. In
the simulation model, this policy package would compress the Bithumb-vs-offshore satoshi-premium
standard deviation toward the offshore range (0.2--0.3\%), and the Korean market would no longer
provide the structural impetus for capital outflow. These implications, however, are conditional
on the calibration assumptions; actual policy decisions should be based on follow-up empirical
work that re-estimates the model on real exchange data (\S 8).

\section{Limitations and Follow-Up Research Agenda}

\paragraph{Limitation 1: synthetic-data nature.}
All quantitative figures rest on synthetic series. Sample size, the exact location and amplitude
of events, and parameter estimates ($\beta_1=1.40$, $\hat\gamma=-0.073$, the $\rho_X$ values) depend
on calibration assumptions. The first priority of follow-up empirical work is to re-run the
identical pipeline on real one-minute or one-day data from the Bithumb, Binance, OKX, and Bybit
public APIs.

\paragraph{Limitation 2: limited cross-sectional control in the $\mathrm{H_{BSC}}$ test.}
The normalised variance ratio $\rho_X$ controls only for own-price volatility, leaving market cap,
trading volume, governance events, and fundamentals uncontrolled. BSC and ERC-20 tokens may be
different \emph{types} of asset, and a selection-bias concern arises.

\paragraph{Follow-up agenda 1: BTCB vs WBTC natural experiment.}
The cleanest test of $\mathrm{H_{BSC}}$ would compare two chain-versions of the same asset. BTCB
(BTC on BSC) and WBTC (BTC on Ethereum) both target a 1:1 peg and have essentially identical
fundamentals. If both were listed on Bithumb and Binance, a comparison of $\rho_{\text{BTCB}}$ and
$\rho_{\text{WBTC}}$ would deliver a clean ``chain effect'' identification.

\paragraph{Follow-up agenda 2: panel analysis across many BSC tokens.}
Extending the analysis to CAKE, BAKE, ALPACA, ALPHA, and others listed on Bithumb or Upbit would
allow a panel decomposition of $\rho_X$ on token characteristics (market cap, liquidity, Korean
venue share).

\paragraph{Follow-up agenda 3: explicit analysis of the Travel Rule's effect.}
Re-examining the Travel-Rule channel constraint documented by Hwang et al.\ (2024) within the
satoshi-premium framework would allow a regime-switching model with March 25, 2022 as the
structural break.

\paragraph{Follow-up agenda 4: DEX--CEX microstructure analysis.}
A structural estimation built on Capponi \& Jia (2021)---explicitly modelling PancakeSwap LP
incentives and Korean retail's DEX-access cost---would quantitatively decompose the mechanism of
$\mathrm{H_{BSC}}$ rejection.

\paragraph{Follow-up agenda 5: behavioural-economic interpretation of panic/euphoria asymmetry.}
If the leverage term $\gamma<0$ proves robust in both simulation and real data, it would suggest
that the loss aversion and disposition effect of Korean retail manifest as premium asymmetry at
macro shock times. Combined with the investor-type decomposition of Ahn \& Kim (2023), this could
provide a behavioural-economic microfoundation.

\paragraph{Limitation 3: EGARCH distributional assumption and winsorisation.}
The Student-$t$ estimate $\hat\nu=37.4$ is essentially Gaussian, an artefact of $\pm 20\%$
winsorisation. Applying the GED distribution or Hansen's (1994) skewed-$t$ to the raw data may
capture fatter tails. Robustness checks using GJR-GARCH or EGARCH(2,1) and LR/AIC/BIC comparisons
are also recommended.

\paragraph{Follow-up agenda 6: duration analysis of no-arbitrage-band exits.}
The interpretive ambiguity of $\rho_{\text{BNB}}>\rho_{\text{XVS}}$ (\S 7) may be sharpened by
focussing on a \emph{duration} hypothesis rather than a point-in-time hypothesis. Comparing
the distributions of time-above-threshold (e.g., a 5\% fair-premium threshold) for the two
hypotheses---``DEX access barrier'' (long-tail) vs ``microstructure noise''
(frequent-but-short)---would deliver discriminating power. Survival analysis or a Hawkes process
fit would be appropriate tools.

\paragraph{Follow-up agenda 7: empirical verification of the offshore-exchange spread.}
The identification strategy in \S\ref{sec:cross} relies on the assumption that OKX and Bybit are
modelled by pure microstructure noise without regulatory friction. Verifying this assumption is a
core task: (i) collect synchronous one-minute BTC data from Binance, OKX, and Bybit and compute
pairwise satoshi premia empirically; (ii) verify whether the order-of-magnitude difference vs
Bithumb-involved pairs is observed in practice. If empirical magnitudes match the simulation, the
policy implications of this paper acquire strong empirical support.

\paragraph{Data and code availability.}
All synthetic data generation, charts, tables, and OLS/HAC/EGARCH estimation procedures are written
in Python and released in reproducible form. The full dataset (CSV), figures (PNG), and build
scripts are available in the GitHub repository
\href{https://github.com/gameworkerkim/vibe-investing}{\texttt{github.com/gameworkerkim/vibe-investing}},
in the \texttt{Paper} subdirectory.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper redefines the price divergence between Bithumb and Binance using the BTC-num\'eraire
satoshi premium $\pi^{\text{BTC}}_X(t)$, illustrating the construction with calibrated simulation
evidence using BNB and XVS (both listed on Bithumb). The measure removes, at the measurement stage,
the macro noise---FX, capital controls, stablecoin premium, remittance delays---that is bundled
into the conventional KRW measure, isolating the pure question of ``how much more expensive is
token $X$ relative to BTC, inside Bithumb, than outside''.

Simulation findings indicate (i) FX-noise removal of approximately one-half of the KRW-premium
standard deviation; (ii) tentative rejection of $\mathrm{H_{BSC}}$, with the normalised
BSC-side premium variance exceeding the ETH baseline; (iii) loss of significance for the Korean
regulatory dummy under HAC but strong significance for the global-shock dummy; and (iv) a
significant negative EGARCH leverage term, supporting asymmetric volatility.

The contributions are two-fold. First, the BTC-num\'eraire measure offers a new
\emph{measurement tool} whose statistical superiority is established theoretically by
Proposition 1. Second, the BSC triangular structure and the normalised variance ratio $\rho_X$ form
a new \emph{testing tool}. These tools can be applied directly in exchange listing advisory,
arbitrage system design, and---policy-wise---in the kimchi-premium monitoring activities of
financial authorities such as the FSC and the KoFIU.

\paragraph{Policy direction suggested by the simulation.}
Through the cross-exchange comparison (\S\ref{sec:cross}), this paper provides simulation-level
evidence that the kimchi premium appears closer to ``the price of a Korea-specific regulatory
friction'' than to ``a general market inefficiency''. The order-of-magnitude difference---0.2--0.3\%
standard deviation among Binance, OKX, and Bybit pairs vs 0.9--1.6\% for Bithumb-involved pairs in
the simulation---demonstrates \emph{conceptually} that the premium nearly disappears in markets
where the arbitrage channel is alive. The detected illegal-forex statistic (approximately 14.2
trillion KRW over nine years; approximately 2 trillion KRW over 2024--2025) can be read as a lower
bound on the economic cost the premium imposes on Korea. A policy package combining
\emph{maintaining} the strength of the KYC/AML/CFT framework with \emph{gradual} liberalisation
of foreign-user access and \emph{building} KRW-stablecoin infrastructure indicates, within the
simulation model, a direction in which the kimchi premium would be structurally compressed.
\emph{Actual policy decisions should be informed by follow-up empirical work that re-estimates
the present model on real API data from Bithumb, Binance, OKX, and Bybit.}

\paragraph{Core modelling assumption --- offshore-exchange spread.}
The cross-exchange comparison result (\S\ref{sec:cross}) rests on the following \emph{decisive}
assumption. The OKX and Bybit price series are generated by adding only AR(1) microstructure noise
to the Binance price ($\phi=0.55$, $\sigma_\varepsilon \in [0.0012,\, 0.0016]$). That is, the

without regulatory friction''. Consequently, the 0.2--0.3\% standard deviation of the
offshore-only premium in the simulation is \emph{partly} a direct function of this assumption. We
nevertheless argue that the assumption is a reasonable first-order approximation, because (i) the
BTC-price difference between Binance and OKX in real-exchange data is typically below 50 basis
points (Pieters \& Vivanco, 2017), and (ii) capital can move between non-Korean major exchanges
without systemic delay. Verification of this assumption is the priority of follow-up empirical
research (\S 8 Agenda 7).
